# Organisation of Working Time Act 1997 (Ireland)

## Overview
The Organisation of Working Time Act 1997 (OWTA) is the primary Irish legislation governing working hours, rest periods, and annual leave entitlements for employees. It implements the EU Working Time Directive (2003/88/EC) into Irish law.

**Effective Date:** 30 September 1997
**Jurisdiction:** Ireland
**Enforced by:** Workplace Relations Commission (WRC)

---

## Maximum Working Hours

### Section 15 — Maximum Weekly Working Hours
An employer shall not permit an employee to work, in each period of 7 days, more than an average of **48 hours**. This average is calculated over a reference period of:
- **4 months** (default)
- **6 months** (for specified sectors including agriculture, tourism, and hospitals)
- **12 months** (where agreed by collective agreement approved by the Labour Court)

**Note:** This is a hard cap — US-style exempt/non-exempt classifications do not apply. All employees are covered regardless of salary level.

### Section 16 — Nightly Working Hours
Night workers shall not work more than an average of **8 hours** in each 24-hour period, calculated over a 2-month reference period. Night work is defined as work performed between **midnight and 7:00 AM**.

---

## Rest Periods

### Section 11 — Daily Rest
An employee is entitled to a rest period of not less than **11 consecutive hours** in each period of 24 hours.

### Section 12 — Rest Breaks
An employee is entitled to a rest break of:
- **15 minutes** after 4.5 hours of work
- **30 minutes** after 6 hours of work (which may include the first 15-minute break)

### Section 13 — Weekly Rest
An employee is entitled to a rest period of not less than **24 consecutive hours** in each period of 7 days, preceded by a daily rest period of 11 hours. In practice, this means **35 consecutive hours** off per week.

---

## Annual Leave

### Section 19 — Annual Leave Entitlement
An employee is entitled to paid annual leave equal to:
- **4 working weeks** in a leave year in which the employee works at least 1,365 hours, OR
- **One-third of a working week** for each month in which the employee works at least 117 hours, OR
- **8% of the hours worked** in a leave year, subject to a maximum of 4 working weeks

**Key differences from US practice:**
- This is a **statutory minimum** — there is no concept of "unlimited PTO" replacing this entitlement
- Annual leave **accrues** from the start of employment — no waiting periods
- Employees **cannot waive** their annual leave entitlement
- Annual leave must generally be taken in the year it is earned (carry-over may be limited)

### Section 20 — Times and Pay for Annual Leave
- The employer determines the timing of annual leave, having regard to work requirements and the employee's need for rest and recreation
- Annual leave must be paid at the employee's **normal weekly rate**
- An employee may request a specific period of leave and must be given at least **1 month's notice** of the approved dates

### Section 23 — Payment in Lieu on Cessation
On cessation of employment, an employee is entitled to payment in lieu of any annual leave accrued but not taken. This right is **non-waivable**.

## Public Holidays

### Section 21 & Second Schedule — Public Holidays
Ireland has **10 public holidays** (as of 2024):
1. New Year's Day (1 January)
2. St. Brigid's Day (first Monday in February)
3. St. Patrick's Day (17 March)
4. Easter Monday
5. First Monday in May
6. First Monday in June
7. First Monday in August
8. Last Monday in October
9. Christmas Day (25 December)
10. St. Stephen's Day (26 December)

### Employee Entitlements on Public Holidays
An employee (who has worked for at least 40 hours in the 5 weeks preceding the public holiday) is entitled to one of the following:
- A paid day off on the public holiday
- A paid day off within a month
- An additional day of annual leave
- An additional day's pay

**Part-time employees** who do not normally work on the day the public holiday falls are entitled to **one-fifth of their normal weekly pay**.

## Zero-Hours and "If-and-When" Contracts

### Section 18 — Zero-Hours Contracts
Where an employee is required to be available for work but is not called in, the employee is entitled to compensation of **25% of the hours** they were required to be available (or 15 hours, whichever is less).

**Note:** The Employment (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2018 further restricts zero-hours practices by requiring employers to provide employees with a written statement of their core working hours within 5 days of commencing employment.

---

## Records and Enforcement

### Section 25 — Records
Employers must keep records of:
- Employee start and finish times
- Hours worked each day and each week
- Leave granted (annual leave and public holidays)

Records must be retained for **3 years**.

### Section 27 — Complaints to the WRC
An employee may present a complaint to the WRC within **6 months** of the contravention (extendable to 12 months for reasonable cause). The WRC may award compensation of up to **2 years' remuneration**.

## Key Compliance Points for Policy Migration
1. Remove any references to **"exempt" or "non-exempt" employee classifications** — all employees are covered
2. Replace US overtime rules with the **48-hour weekly maximum** and rest period requirements
3. Ensure minimum **4 weeks annual leave** — US policies offering less must be upgraded
4. Add all **10 Irish public holidays** — replace US federal holidays
5. Remove "unlimited PTO" language — must guarantee the statutory minimum
6. Add mandatory **rest break provisions** (15 min after 4.5 hours, 30 min after 6 hours)
7. Ensure **daily 11-hour rest** and **weekly 35-hour rest** are referenced
8. Remove any "use it or lose it" annual leave policies that eliminate accrued but unused leave — Irish law requires payment in lieu on termination
