# Data Protection Act 2018 (Ireland)

## Overview
The Data Protection Act 2018 is the Irish legislation that supplements and gives further effect to the GDPR in Ireland. It exercises certain derogations permitted under the GDPR and establishes the Data Protection Commission (DPC) as Ireland's supervisory authority.

**Effective Date:** 24 May 2018
**Jurisdiction:** Ireland
**Primary Authority:** Data Protection Commission (DPC)

---

## Relationship with the GDPR
The Act does not replace the GDPR but operates alongside it. Where the GDPR permits member states to legislate further, this Act provides the Irish-specific rules. Key areas where the Act supplements the GDPR include:
- Age of consent for children's data
- Processing for journalistic, academic, artistic, and literary purposes
- Processing of special categories of data in employment
- Restrictions on data subject rights for specific purposes

---

## Key Provisions

### Section 29 — Age of Digital Consent
The age at which a child can consent to the processing of their personal data in relation to information society services (e.g., social media) in Ireland is **16 years** (the GDPR allows member states to set this between 13 and 16).

### Section 30 — Restriction of Data Subject Rights
Data subject rights (access, rectification, erasure, restriction, portability, objection) may be restricted where necessary and proportionate to safeguard:
- National security
- Defence
- Public security
- Prevention, detection, investigation, or prosecution of criminal offences
- Important objectives of general public interest
- Protection of judicial independence and proceedings

### Section 36 — Processing of Special Categories of Data in Employment
Employers may process special categories of personal data (health data, trade union membership, etc.) where processing is necessary for:
- Carrying out obligations or exercising rights in the field of employment law
- Reasons of substantial public interest
- Preventive or occupational medicine, assessment of working capacity, medical diagnosis

**Safeguards required:** Suitable and specific measures to safeguard the fundamental rights and interests of the data subject, including confidentiality obligations, limitations on access, and data minimisation.

### Section 37 — Processing for Health Purposes
Processing of health data is permitted where necessary for:
- The provision of health or social care
- The management of health or social care systems
- Public health purposes

### Section 39 — Processing of Data Relating to Criminal Convictions
Processing of personal data relating to criminal convictions and offences may only be carried out:
- Under the control of official authority, OR
- When authorised by EU or member state law providing appropriate safeguards

**Employment context:** Irish employers generally cannot require blanket disclosure of spent convictions. The National Vetting Bureau (Children and Vulnerable Persons) Acts 2012 to 2016 governs vetting for positions involving children or vulnerable persons.

---

## Data Protection Commission (DPC)

### Sections 10-18 — Establishment and Powers
The DPC is Ireland's independent supervisory authority under the GDPR. Key powers include:
- Investigating complaints from data subjects
- Conducting audits and inspections
- Issuing enforcement notices and administrative fines
- Approving codes of conduct and certification mechanisms
- Acting as lead supervisory authority for cross-border processing (Ireland is the EU headquarters for many major tech companies)

### Section 115 — Offences
It is a criminal offence to:
- Obtain or disclose personal data without authorisation
- Obstruct or fail to cooperate with the DPC
- Fail to comply with an enforcement notice

Penalties include fines of up to **€250,000** on summary conviction or **€1,000,000** on conviction on indictment.

## Employment-Specific Provisions

### Lawful Basis in Employment
- **Contract of employment** (Article 6(1)(b) GDPR) — processing personal data necessary for the employment contract
- **Legal obligation** (Article 6(1)(c) GDPR) — payroll, tax, health and safety obligations
- **Legitimate interests** (Article 6(1)(f) GDPR) — must pass the three-part test (purpose, necessity, balancing)
- **Consent** — generally not appropriate in the employment context due to the power imbalance (per DPC guidance)

### Employee Data Retention
The following retention periods are commonly applied in Ireland (based on statutory limitation periods):
- **Payroll and tax records:** 6 years (per Revenue requirements)
- **Employment contracts and terms:** 6 years post-employment (Statute of Limitations)
- **Health and safety records:** 10 years
- **Personal injury claims:** 2 years from date of knowledge (Statute of Limitations (Amendment) Act 1991)
- **Equality claims:** 6 years
- **CCTV footage:** typically 28-30 days (per DPC guidance)

---

## Cross-Border Processing and the DPC as Lead Authority
As Ireland hosts the European headquarters of many multinational technology companies (e.g., Meta, Google, Apple, Microsoft, TikTok), the DPC serves as the lead supervisory authority for cross-border data processing complaints involving these companies under the GDPR's one-stop-shop mechanism (Article 56 GDPR).

---

## Key Compliance Points for Policy Migration
1. Set children's digital consent age to **16 years** (not 13 as in some US frameworks)
2. Ensure employee data processing relies on appropriate legal bases — **avoid relying on consent in employment**
3. Apply Irish-specific **retention periods** — US retention schedules may differ significantly
4. Reference the **DPC** as the relevant supervisory authority, not US regulators
5. Criminal background check policies must comply with **National Vetting Bureau Acts** — no blanket disclosure requirements
6. Special category data processing in employment must include **specific safeguards** per Section 36
7. Include provisions for **cooperation with the DPC** in data protection policies
