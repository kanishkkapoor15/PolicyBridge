# EU AI Act — EU Regulation 2024/1689

## Overview
The EU AI Act is the world's first comprehensive legal framework for artificial intelligence. It establishes a risk-based approach to regulating AI systems, with requirements proportionate to the level of risk an AI system poses to health, safety, and fundamental rights.

**Adopted:** 13 June 2024
**Entry into Force:** 1 August 2024
**Full Application:** 2 August 2026 (phased implementation)
**Jurisdiction:** All EU member states

---

## Risk Classification Framework

### Unacceptable Risk — Prohibited Practices (Article 5)
The following AI practices are prohibited from 2 February 2025:
- **Subliminal manipulation** — AI systems deploying subliminal techniques beyond a person's consciousness to materially distort behaviour causing significant harm
- **Exploitation of vulnerabilities** — AI targeting specific groups (age, disability, social/economic situation) to materially distort behaviour
- **Social scoring** — AI systems evaluating or classifying natural persons based on social behaviour or personal characteristics, leading to detrimental treatment
- **Real-time remote biometric identification** in publicly accessible spaces for law enforcement (with limited exceptions)
- **Emotion recognition** in workplaces and educational institutions (with limited exceptions for safety/medical purposes)
- **Untargeted scraping of facial images** from the internet or CCTV to build facial recognition databases
- **Biometric categorisation** based on sensitive characteristics (race, political opinions, trade union membership, religious beliefs, sexual orientation)

### High-Risk AI Systems (Article 6, Annex III)
AI systems in the following areas are classified as high-risk:
1. **Biometrics** — remote biometric identification, biometric categorisation, emotion recognition
2. **Critical infrastructure** — safety components of road traffic, water/gas/heating/electricity supply
3. **Education and vocational training** — determining access to education, evaluating learning outcomes, monitoring prohibited behaviour during tests
4. **Employment, workers management, and access to self-employment:**
   - Recruitment and selection (CV screening, interview analysis)
   - Decisions on promotion, termination, task allocation
   - Monitoring and evaluating performance and behaviour
5. **Access to essential services** — credit scoring, insurance pricing, emergency services dispatch
6. **Law enforcement** — risk assessment, polygraphs, evidence reliability assessment, crime prediction
7. **Migration, asylum, and border control** — risk assessment, document verification
8. **Administration of justice** — researching and interpreting facts and law, applying law to facts

### Limited Risk (Article 50) — Transparency Obligations
- AI systems interacting with humans (chatbots) must disclose that they are AI
- Emotion recognition and biometric categorisation systems must inform users
- AI-generated or manipulated content (deepfakes) must be labelled as artificially generated

### Minimal Risk
- No specific obligations beyond voluntary codes of conduct
- Examples: spam filters, AI in video games

## Requirements for High-Risk AI Systems

### Article 9 — Risk Management System
A continuous, iterative risk management process must be established, including:
- Identification and analysis of known and reasonably foreseeable risks
- Estimation and evaluation of risks from intended use and reasonably foreseeable misuse
- Adoption of appropriate risk management measures
- Testing to identify the most appropriate risk management measures

### Article 10 — Data and Data Governance
Training, validation, and testing data sets must be:
- Subject to appropriate data governance and management practices
- Relevant, sufficiently representative, and to the best extent possible free of errors and complete
- Subject to examination for possible biases that may lead to discrimination

### Article 11 — Technical Documentation
Comprehensive technical documentation must be drawn up before the system is placed on the market, including:
- General description, intended purpose, and intended users
- Detailed description of elements and development process
- Information about monitoring, functioning, and control
- Risk management documentation
- Description of any changes made during the lifecycle

### Article 13 — Transparency and Information to Deployers
High-risk AI systems must be designed to enable deployers to interpret the output and use it appropriately.

### Article 14 — Human Oversight
High-risk AI systems must be designed to allow effective human oversight during use, including:
- The ability to fully understand the system's capacities and limitations
- The ability to correctly interpret outputs
- The ability to decide not to use the system or override/reverse its output
- The ability to intervene or interrupt the system

### Article 22 — Automated Individual Decision-Making
Where a high-risk AI system is used to make or substantially influence decisions about natural persons, the deployer must:
- Inform the affected person that they are subject to the system
- Provide meaningful explanations of the role of the system in the decision
- Enable the person to contest the decision and request human review

---

## Obligations for Deployers (Article 26)

Organisations that deploy (use) high-risk AI systems must:
1. Assign **human oversight** to natural persons with the necessary competence, training, and authority
2. Ensure input data is **relevant and sufficiently representative**
3. Monitor the operation and report any **serious incidents** or malfunctioning
4. Conduct a **fundamental rights impact assessment** before putting the system into use (for public bodies and certain private entities)
5. Inform **workers' representatives and affected workers** before deploying a high-risk AI system in the workplace
6. Inform natural persons that they are subject to **automated decision-making**

---

## General-Purpose AI (GPAI) Models (Articles 51-56)

### All GPAI Models
- Must provide technical documentation to downstream providers
- Must have a policy for respecting EU copyright law
- Must publish a detailed summary of training data content

### GPAI Models with Systemic Risk
- Must perform model evaluations including adversarial testing
- Must assess and mitigate possible systemic risks
- Must report serious incidents to the AI Office
- Must ensure adequate cybersecurity protections

---

## Enforcement and Penalties (Article 99)

- **Prohibited AI practices:** Up to **€35,000,000 or 7% of total worldwide annual turnover**
- **High-risk AI non-compliance:** Up to **€15,000,000 or 3% of total worldwide annual turnover**
- **Incorrect information to authorities:** Up to **€7,500,000 or 1% of total worldwide annual turnover**
- **SMEs and startups:** Proportionate caps apply

---

## Timeline
- **2 February 2025:** Prohibitions on unacceptable risk AI apply
- **2 August 2025:** GPAI model obligations apply; governance structure established
- **2 August 2026:** Full application of high-risk AI requirements
- **2 August 2027:** High-risk AI systems in Annex I (regulated products) apply

---

## Key Compliance Points for Policy Migration
1. **AI usage policies** must classify AI systems used by the organisation according to the risk framework
2. **HR/Employment AI** (recruitment tools, performance monitoring, CV screening) is **high-risk** — requires full compliance
3. **Emotion recognition in the workplace** is **prohibited** (from February 2025)
4. Policies must require **human oversight** for AI-assisted employment decisions
5. **Workers must be informed** before AI systems are deployed in the workplace
6. **Transparency obligations** apply to all AI chatbots and content generation tools
7. US-origin AI governance policies must be updated to align with the **risk classification framework**
8. **Fundamental rights impact assessments** may be required before deploying high-risk AI
9. **Article 22 (GDPR) alignment** — automated decision-making provisions must be consistent across both regulations
