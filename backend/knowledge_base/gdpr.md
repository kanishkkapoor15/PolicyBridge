# General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) — EU Regulation 2016/679

## Overview
The GDPR is the primary EU regulation governing the processing of personal data of individuals within the European Economic Area. It applies to any organisation that processes personal data of EU residents, regardless of where the organisation is based.

**Effective Date:** 25 May 2018
**Jurisdiction:** All EU/EEA member states, including Ireland
**Enforced by:** Data Protection Commission (DPC) in Ireland

---

## Key Principles (Article 5)

### Article 5(1)(a) — Lawfulness, Fairness and Transparency
Personal data must be processed lawfully, fairly, and in a transparent manner. Organisations must have a valid legal basis for processing and must clearly communicate to data subjects how their data is used.

### Article 5(1)(b) — Purpose Limitation
Personal data must be collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes and not further processed in a manner incompatible with those purposes.

### Article 5(1)(c) — Data Minimisation
Personal data must be adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary in relation to the purposes for which it is processed.

### Article 5(1)(d) — Accuracy
Personal data must be accurate and, where necessary, kept up to date. Reasonable steps must be taken to ensure inaccurate data is erased or rectified without delay.

### Article 5(1)(e) — Storage Limitation
Personal data must be kept in a form that permits identification of data subjects for no longer than is necessary for the purposes for which the data is processed.

### Article 5(1)(f) — Integrity and Confidentiality
Personal data must be processed in a manner that ensures appropriate security, including protection against unauthorised or unlawful processing, accidental loss, destruction, or damage.

### Article 5(2) — Accountability
The data controller must be able to demonstrate compliance with all of the above principles.

---

## Lawful Bases for Processing (Article 6)

### Article 6(1)(a) — Consent
The data subject has given consent to the processing of their personal data for one or more specific purposes. Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous.

### Article 6(1)(b) — Contractual Necessity
Processing is necessary for the performance of a contract to which the data subject is party, or to take steps at the request of the data subject prior to entering into a contract.

### Article 6(1)(c) — Legal Obligation
Processing is necessary for compliance with a legal obligation to which the controller is subject.

### Article 6(1)(d) — Vital Interests
Processing is necessary to protect the vital interests of the data subject or another natural person.

### Article 6(1)(e) — Public Interest
Processing is necessary for the performance of a task carried out in the public interest or in the exercise of official authority.

### Article 6(1)(f) — Legitimate Interests
Processing is necessary for the purposes of the legitimate interests pursued by the controller or a third party, except where overridden by the interests or fundamental rights of the data subject. **Note:** This basis is not available to public authorities in the performance of their tasks.

---

## Data Subject Rights

### Article 13 & 14 — Right to Information
Data subjects must be informed about the identity of the controller, purposes of processing, legal basis, recipients, retention periods, and their rights at the time data is collected.

### Article 15 — Right of Access
Data subjects have the right to obtain confirmation of whether their data is being processed and access to a copy of their personal data, free of charge.

### Article 16 — Right to Rectification
Data subjects have the right to have inaccurate personal data rectified without undue delay.

### Article 17 — Right to Erasure ("Right to be Forgotten")
Data subjects have the right to request erasure of their personal data where the data is no longer necessary, consent is withdrawn, or processing is unlawful.

### Article 18 — Right to Restriction of Processing
Data subjects can request restriction of processing where accuracy is contested, processing is unlawful, or the data is no longer needed but required for legal claims.

### Article 20 — Right to Data Portability
Data subjects have the right to receive their personal data in a structured, commonly used, and machine-readable format and to transmit it to another controller.

### Article 21 — Right to Object
Data subjects have the right to object to processing based on legitimate interests or public interest, including profiling. For direct marketing, the right to object is absolute.

### Article 22 — Automated Decision-Making
Data subjects have the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing, including profiling, which produces legal effects or similarly significantly affects them. Exceptions exist where the decision is necessary for a contract, authorised by law, or based on explicit consent.

---

## Data Protection by Design and by Default (Article 25)
Controllers must implement appropriate technical and organisational measures to ensure that, by default, only personal data necessary for each specific purpose is processed. This applies to the amount of data collected, the extent of processing, the period of storage, and accessibility.

---

## Data Protection Impact Assessments (Article 35)
A DPIA is required where processing is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of individuals. This includes:
- Systematic and extensive profiling with significant effects
- Large-scale processing of special categories of data
- Systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas

---

## Data Breach Notification

### Article 33 — Notification to Supervisory Authority
The controller must notify the DPC of a personal data breach within **72 hours** of becoming aware of it, unless the breach is unlikely to result in a risk to individuals' rights and freedoms.

### Article 34 — Communication to Data Subject
Where a breach is likely to result in a **high risk** to the rights and freedoms of individuals, the controller must communicate the breach to the data subject without undue delay.

---

## International Data Transfers (Chapter V)

### Article 44-49 — Transfers Outside the EEA
Personal data may only be transferred to third countries or international organisations where:
- An adequacy decision exists (Article 45) — e.g., EU-US Data Privacy Framework
- Appropriate safeguards are in place (Article 46) — e.g., Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs), Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)
- A derogation applies (Article 49) — e.g., explicit consent, contractual necessity

**Key requirement for US transfers:** Following the Schrems II judgment, organisations must conduct a Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA) when relying on SCCs to transfer data to the US or other countries without an adequacy decision.

---

## Data Protection Officer (Articles 37-39)
A DPO must be appointed where:
- Processing is carried out by a public authority
- Core activities require regular and systematic monitoring of data subjects on a large scale
- Core activities involve large-scale processing of special categories of data

The DPO must be provided with resources, must not receive instructions regarding the exercise of their tasks, and reports directly to the highest management level.

---

## Employment Context — Key Considerations
- **Employee consent** is generally not considered freely given due to the power imbalance in the employment relationship (Recital 43). Employers should rely on other legal bases such as contractual necessity or legitimate interests.
- **Employee monitoring** must comply with Article 5 principles and may require a DPIA under Article 35.
- **Special categories of data** (Article 9) — health data, trade union membership, and biometric data — require additional protections in the employment context.
- **Retention of employee data** must be clearly defined and limited to what is necessary (commonly 7 years post-employment for tax/legal purposes in Ireland, but varies by data type).

---

## Penalties (Article 83)
- Up to **€10 million or 2% of annual global turnover** (whichever is greater) for infringements of controller/processor obligations
- Up to **€20 million or 4% of annual global turnover** (whichever is greater) for infringements of data subject rights, lawful bases, or international transfer requirements
