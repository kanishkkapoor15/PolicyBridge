# Data Protection Commission (DPC) Ireland — Guidance Summaries

## Overview
The Data Protection Commission (DPC) is Ireland's independent national authority responsible for upholding the fundamental right of individuals to have their personal data protected. The DPC publishes guidance notes, codes of practice, and regulatory decisions that provide practical interpretation of the GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018 in the Irish context.

**Website:** dataprotection.ie
**Established Under:** Data Protection Act 2018, Section 10

---

## DPC Guidance on Employment and Data Protection

### Employee Monitoring in the Workplace
The DPC has published guidance on the limits of employee monitoring:

**Key Principles:**
- Employers must have a **clear, legitimate purpose** for monitoring (e.g., preventing fraud, ensuring compliance with policy, protecting IT systems)
- Monitoring must be **proportionate** — blanket surveillance of all employee activity is unlikely to be justified
- Employees must be **informed in advance** of any monitoring, including the nature, extent, and purposes
- A **Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)** should be conducted before implementing monitoring systems
- **Covert monitoring** is only permissible in exceptional circumstances (e.g., suspected criminal activity) and must be strictly time-limited

**Types of Monitoring Covered:**
- Email and internet usage monitoring
- CCTV in the workplace
- GPS tracking of company vehicles
- Keystroke logging and screen capture
- Call recording
- Access badge/swipe card monitoring

**DPC Recommendation:** Employers should adopt a clearly documented **Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)** for IT systems and make employees aware of what monitoring takes place, what data is collected, and how long it is retained.

### CCTV in the Workplace
- CCTV must not be used in areas where employees have a **reasonable expectation of privacy** (e.g., bathrooms, changing rooms, break rooms)
- **Signage** must be displayed indicating that CCTV is in operation, the identity of the controller, and the purpose
- Footage should typically be retained for **no more than 28-30 days** unless required for a specific investigation
- Access to footage must be **restricted** to authorised personnel
- A DPIA should be conducted

### Employee Email and Internet Monitoring
- Employers should not routinely access the **content** of employee emails
- If monitoring of internet usage is necessary, it should focus on **aggregate data** (e.g., bandwidth usage, categories of sites visited) rather than individual browsing histories
- Employees must be informed of the monitoring through the AUP
- **Personal use policies** should clearly state whether limited personal use is permitted and what monitoring applies

## DPC Guidance on Data Subject Access Requests (DSARs)

### Handling DSARs in Employment
- Employers must respond to DSARs within **1 month** (extendable by 2 months for complex requests)
- The response must include **all personal data** held about the individual, including emails, HR files, performance reviews, disciplinary records, and CCTV footage
- **Third-party data** in documents (e.g., other employees named in emails) must be redacted or balanced against the third party's rights
- Employers **cannot refuse** a DSAR merely because it is inconvenient or burdensome
- Employers can request clarification if the request is **manifestly unfounded or excessive** (Article 12(5) GDPR)
- The response must be provided **free of charge** (a reasonable fee may only be charged for manifestly unfounded or excessive requests, or for additional copies)

### DPC Enforcement Examples
- The DPC has found against employers who failed to provide complete DSAR responses
- Failure to include CCTV footage or internal emails referencing the data subject in a DSAR response has been found to be non-compliant

## DPC Guidance on Data Breaches

### What Constitutes a Data Breach
A personal data breach is any breach of security leading to the accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorised disclosure of, or access to personal data. This includes:
- Sending personal data to the wrong recipient (email misdirection)
- Loss or theft of devices containing personal data
- Unauthorised access to HR records or payroll systems
- Ransomware attacks encrypting personal data
- Accidental deletion of data without backup

### DPC Breach Notification Requirements
- Notify the DPC within **72 hours** using the DPC's online breach notification form
- Notification must include: nature of the breach, categories and approximate numbers of data subjects affected, likely consequences, measures taken or proposed
- If the breach is likely to result in a **high risk** to individuals, the data subjects must also be notified directly
- The DPC must be notified of **all breaches** unless the controller can demonstrate the breach is **unlikely to result in a risk** to individuals' rights and freedoms

### DPC Record-Keeping
- All breaches must be recorded in an **internal breach register**, regardless of whether they are notified to the DPC
- The register must include facts, effects, and remedial actions taken

## DPC Guidance on Direct Marketing

### Electronic Marketing
- Subject to the **ePrivacy Regulations (S.I. No. 336 of 2011)** — consent is required for electronic direct marketing (email, SMS, automated calls)
- **Soft opt-in** applies only where: (1) the contact details were obtained in the context of a sale or negotiation, (2) the marketing relates to similar products/services, and (3) the recipient is given a clear opportunity to opt out at the time of collection and in every subsequent message
- **B2B marketing** to corporate emails also requires consent under Irish ePrivacy rules (unlike some other jurisdictions)
- The DPC conducts audits and has issued significant fines for unsolicited marketing communications

## DPC Guidance on International Data Transfers

### Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)
- The DPC recommends using the **EU Commission's June 2021 SCCs** for transfers to third countries
- A **Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA)** must be conducted to evaluate whether the legal framework of the third country provides adequate protection
- **Supplementary measures** may be required (e.g., encryption, pseudonymisation, contractual provisions)

### US Data Transfers
- Following the EU-US Data Privacy Framework (DPF) adequacy decision (July 2023), transfers to DPF-certified US organisations may proceed without SCCs
- For non-DPF-certified US entities, SCCs plus TIA remain required
- The DPC monitors compliance and has initiated investigations into international transfer practices

## DPC Codes of Practice

### Data Protection in the Workplace
- Published guidance on creating workplace data protection policies
- Recommends annual **data protection training** for all staff
- Emphasises the importance of **data protection by design** in HR processes
- Requires clear **privacy notices** for employees at the time of hiring

### Children's Data
- Strict rules on processing children's data (under 16 in Ireland)
- Enhanced transparency and parental consent requirements
- Relevant for organisations employing apprentices or work experience participants under 16

## Key Compliance Points for Policy Migration
1. **Employee monitoring policies** must meet DPC proportionality requirements — blanket surveillance is not permitted
2. **CCTV policies** must include signage requirements, 28-30 day retention, and DPIA
3. **DSAR procedures** must be updated to meet 1-month response deadline and include all data categories
4. **Breach notification procedures** must include 72-hour DPC notification and internal breach register
5. **Marketing policies** must comply with Irish ePrivacy Regulations — consent required for electronic marketing
6. **International transfer policies** must reference SCCs, TIAs, and the EU-US DPF where applicable
7. **Privacy notices** for employees must be provided at time of hiring
8. **Annual data protection training** should be mandated in policy
